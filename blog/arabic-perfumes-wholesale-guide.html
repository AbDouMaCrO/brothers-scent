Arabic Perfumes Wholesale: Oud and Oriental Fragrances for GCC Retailers

The complete sourcing guide for oud, bakhoor, attar, and oriental EDP — what to stock, which brands to buy, and how demand shifts across UAE, Saudi, and Kuwait.

BrotherScents July 12, 2025 8 min read

Oud is the language of the Arabian Peninsula. Long before French perfumers discovered the resinous heartwood of Aquilaria trees, Gulf families were burning bakhoor at weddings, passing around vials of pure oud oil, and selecting the deepest, richest oriental compositions as statements of status and identity. For any fragrance retailer operating in the GCC — whether in Dubai, Riyadh, Kuwait City, or Muscat — Arabic and oriental fragrances are not a niche subcategory. They are the foundation of the market.

This guide covers everything wholesale buyers need to know about sourcing Arabic fragrances in 2025: the major brands, the product categories, the pricing dynamics, and — crucially — how demand differs across markets within the GCC itself.

Understanding the Arabic Fragrance Market

The GCC fragrance market was valued at over USD 2.5 billion in 2024, with Arabic and oriental compositions accounting for an estimated 40–50% of unit volume. This is not surprising: oud, rose, amber, musk, and saffron are culturally embedded scent references for Gulf consumers in a way that has no parallel in Western markets.

What has changed in the last five years is the emergence of a new generation of Arabic fragrance consumers who are simultaneously loyal to traditional formats (oud oil, bakhoor) and enthusiastic about modern oriental EDPs and niche hybrid compositions that blend Arabic DNA with Western structure. This dual demand profile is great news for retailers: it means you can stock across a price range from AED 30 bakhoor sachets to AED 2,000 premium oud oils and all of it sells.

Product Categories — What to Stock

Oud Oil (Attar)

Pure oud oil — also called attar or dahn al oud — is the most traditional and highest-value Arabic fragrance product. Genuine Cambodian or Indian oud oil commands premium prices (AED 500–5,000+ per tola) but offers extraordinary margin for retailers who build the expertise to sell it. A key selling skill here is explaining provenance: Cambodian oud is smoother and sweeter, while Hindi oud is earthier and more barnyard. Customers who understand the difference become loyal high-spenders.

For wholesale buyers new to oud oil, start with good-quality blended oud oils and attars in the AED 100–500 range. These move fast and provide accessible entry points for consumers exploring the category.

Oriental EDP and EDT

The largest volume category in Arabic fragrances by far. These are alcohol-based perfumes in the familiar spray format, but built around oriental ingredients: oud, amber, rose, saffron, sandalwood, vetiver, and musk. They appeal to a much broader audience than pure oud oil and are the backbone of every Arabic perfumery's revenue.

Bakhoor

Scented wood chips burned on charcoal or an electric burner to perfume the home and clothing. This is a category with very high repeat purchase rates — consumers burn bakhoor regularly and restock frequently. Margin is excellent (often 60–80%) and storage is simple. For Ramadan season, bakhoor sales can triple your normal volume in a single month.

Mabthouth and Muattar

Loose incense blends and pre-mixed bakhoor compositions. Popular in Saudi Arabia and Kuwait especially. If you're serving those markets, carry a range of these alongside standard bakhoor sachets.

The Top Arabic Fragrance Brands for Wholesale

Lattafa Perfumes

Dubai-based Lattafa is arguably the most commercially successful Arabic fragrance brand in the world right now. Their range spans from accessible oriental EDPs (Khamrah, Oud For Glory, Asad) to more sophisticated compositions (Bade'e Al Oud series). Lattafa prices at affordable levels, moves at high velocity, and has massive social media followings globally. For any GCC retailer, Lattafa is non-negotiable. Stock broadly across their range — at least 10–15 SKUs — and replenish frequently.

Al Haramain Perfumes

One of the oldest and most respected Arabic fragrance houses, established in Mecca. Al Haramain is synonymous with premium Arabic perfumery and carries enormous brand trust across the entire Muslim world. The Amber Oud series (Gold, Blue, Silver, Black editions) is their commercial powerhouse. The iconic presentation — ornate bottles in rich colours — makes every Al Haramain fragrance inherently giftable. Premium pricing means better per-unit margin; consumers trade up willingly.

Rasasi Perfumes Industry

Founded in Dubai in 1979, Rasasi is one of the UAE's original fragrance manufacturers. Their range bridges traditional oriental and contemporary composition. La Yuqawam, Hawas, Dhan Al Oudh series, and Junoon are consistent bestsellers. Rasasi has strong brand recognition with UAE nationals and is particularly well-regarded in the Northern Emirates.

Ajmal Perfumes

Another heritage UAE house, in operation since 1951. Ajmal is particularly strong in pure oud oil and high-quality attars, alongside their alcohol-based range. The Evoke series and Aurum for women are strong retail performers. Ajmal's heritage positioning allows for premium retail pricing, supporting excellent margin.

Arabian Oud

Saudi Arabia's biggest fragrance retail brand, with hundreds of company-owned stores across the GCC. As a wholesale brand for independent retailers, Arabian Oud products (Kalemat, Musk Tahara, Oud Cambodi) are recognized and trusted by consumers who may have first encountered them in an Arabian Oud store. A strong halo effect makes these easy to sell.

Swiss Arabian Perfumes

Swiss Arabian blends European fragrance craftsmanship with Arabian ingredients and sensibilities. The result is a range that appeals to both traditional Arabic fragrance lovers and consumers with a taste for Western sophistication. Shaghaf Oud Aswad is their flagship; the Hayaa and Watar series have strong followings among younger consumers. Excellent fit for Dubai's cosmopolitan retail market.

Seasonal Demand — Plan Your Stock Around the Calendar

Arabic fragrance sales are more seasonal than the designer category. Understanding these peaks allows you to order wholesale stock in advance and maximize sell-through during high-demand periods.

Ramadan and Eid Al Fitr

The single biggest selling period for Arabic fragrances. Bakhoor sales can increase 3–5x in the month before Eid. Oriental EDP gift sets are in enormous demand, and consumers specifically seek out premium gift packaging during this period. Order your Ramadan stock at least 6–8 weeks before the holy month begins. Late stock means lost revenue — this is the one period where running out costs you the most.

Eid Al Adha

A strong secondary gifting period, particularly in Saudi Arabia. Oud oils and premium bakhoor move well. Less intense than Eid Al Fitr but still significant — plan for a 30–50% sales uplift in the high-end category.

UAE National Day (December 2nd)

The UAE gifting season. Stock up on Arabic fragrances with UAE-themed packaging or premium presentation. Al Haramain limited editions and Ajmal gift sets are particularly popular.

What Sells Where — UAE vs Saudi vs Kuwait

UAE (Dubai & Abu Dhabi)

The most cosmopolitan market. Consumers want a mix of traditional Arabic fragrances and modern oriental EDPs. International tourists actively seek Arabic brands as souvenirs. South Asian expats (Indians, Pakistanis, Bangladeshis) represent a huge segment and tend toward floral orientals and affordable attars. Stock both traditional and contemporary across all price points.

Saudi Arabia

The world's largest per-capita fragrance market by spending. Saudis lean heavily toward intense, rich, deeply oriental compositions — heavier oud, more saffron, more amber. Volume-wise, the kingdom is the biggest driver of pure oud oil sales in the world. Arabian Oud's home market gives them extra brand weight here. Stock heavier, more concentrated compositions for Saudi-focused buyers.

Kuwait

Kuwaitis have perhaps the most sophisticated fragrance palate in the GCC. They are early adopters of niche and unusual compositions. Mix traditional Arabic brands with some experimental niche Arabic releases. The premium and collector segment is particularly strong. Swiss Arabian, Amouage (Oman), and specialty oud oils sell well in Kuwait.
